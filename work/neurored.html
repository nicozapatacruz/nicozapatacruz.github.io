Context

A complex logistics platform — and the people who operate it.

Neurored is a Transportation Management System (TMS) and Supply Chain Management (SCM) platform used by global enterprises like XPO Logistics and Hapag-Lloyd. Operators use it daily to manage freight forwarding, bookings, shipment documentation, and supply chain visibility — all in one place.

I joined the design team in October 2025. My work covers two areas: designing product interfaces for the platform's core workflows, and building and maintaining components in Neu, Neurored's internal Design System — including bridging those components into production code.

Main project

Redesigning Bookings and Shipping Instructions from scratch.

The most significant design project I've owned at Neurored is the full interface redesign of Bookings and Shipping Instructions — two of the most critical and complex workflows in the platform.

A Booking is the reservation of space with an airline, shipping line, or carrier — a container on a vessel, a pallet on a flight. Without a confirmed booking, there's no shipment. Shipping Instructions are the final data package delivered to the carrier to issue the transport documents: Bill of Lading for ocean freight, Air Waybill for air. If these instructions are wrong, documents come out incorrect — and that means fines, delays, and customs problems.

Both workflows sit at the end of a long chain: Customer Quote → Service Quote → Customer Order → Service Order → Booking → Shipping Instructions. By the time an operator reaches Booking, a lot of decisions have already been made. The interface had to reflect that context without overwhelming them.

What I inherited: a functional but poorly designed set of forms built by developers without UX involvement. Correct in terms of fields — but with no hierarchy, no information architecture, and no consideration for the cognitive load of the operator.

I was handed the list of required fields and data for each workflow and asked to design the interface from scratch in Figma, using components from LWC and from the Neu Design System wherever they applied — and creating new ones where nothing existed yet.

Bookings

A record interface that works at every stage of the process.

The Booking interface is a multi-tab record page — one of the most information-dense patterns in the platform. The operator needs to see the booking's status at a glance, navigate between sections of data without losing their place, and take contextual actions depending on where the booking is in its lifecycle.

The key structural decisions I made: a status stepper at the top (Draft → Requested → Pending Confirmation → Confirmed → Amendment → Shipping Instructions → Rejected → Cancelled → Error) that gives the operator an immediate understanding of where the booking stands. Below it, five tabs — General Details, Parties, Cargo Details, Charges, and References — each scoped to a specific domain of information, with a persistent lateral panel for Validations and Events.

Booking record — General Details tab. Status stepper at the top, routing information structured into Receipt, Main Ocean, and Delivery sections.

The Cargo Details tab was the most complex to design. Operators need to manage multiple containers simultaneously, each with its own goods, dangerous goods declarations, reefer configuration, and OOG (Out of Gauge) specifications. I structured it as a summary table at the top — for scanning across containers — and expandable sections below for editing each one individually.

Cargo Details — summary table plus expandable container sections. Each container can have dangerous goods, reefer settings, and OOG details.

Parties tab showing the lateral Events panel — where the Activity Event component was born. Charges tab with inline editing.

References tab — consistent inline editing pattern applied across all list-based sections.

The Activity Event component was born here. Designing the Events panel in the Booking sidebar — where operators see a chronological log of what happened to the booking — made me realise there was no component in the Design System for this pattern. I designed it, documented it, and it became part of Neu.

Shipping Instructions

A 7-step wizard for the most document-critical workflow.

While Bookings is a record page — something the operator returns to and edits over time — Shipping Instructions needed a fundamentally different pattern. The operator fills it in once, at a critical moment, and submits it to generate the transport documents. One mistake means incorrect documents.

I chose a wizard modal with a 7-step progress stepper: General Details → Parties → Cargo Details → References → BL Instructions → Validations → Preview. The stepped structure forces the operator through each section in a logical sequence, reduces the chance of missing something, and makes the current position in the process always visible.

Shipping Instructions — General Details step. The modal wizard keeps the operator focused on one section at a time.

Parties step with Shipper, Consignee, Forwarder, and additional roles. Cargo Details showing container and goods information per container.

References step. BL Instructions step — freight charges, BL type, Bill of Lading clauses, and release settings.

Validations step — errors and warnings shown using the Section Card component with semantic color coding before the operator can proceed to Preview.

Every section reuses components from the Neu Design System — Section Cards for container groupings, inline editing patterns from the Booking design, and the same Parties structure adapted to the SI context. Consistency between Bookings and Shipping Instructions was a deliberate goal: operators who know one should immediately feel at home in the other.

Design System

Components that came from — and feed back into — the product.

Working on Bookings and Shipping Instructions wasn't just a product design project — it directly informed what components the Design System needed. When a pattern appeared multiple times across both interfaces, it became a candidate for a new component. When an existing component didn't quite fit the context, I redesigned it.

My day-to-day also includes maqueting components in the frontend — replacing raw HTML instances across the platform with proper Design System component calls in Lightning Web Components. Working in both Figma and code gives me a perspective most designers don't have: I know exactly what a design decision costs to implement.

Activity Event

Born from the Events panel in Booking. A chronological component for activity feeds, audit logs, and case histories, with a semantic state system — base, info, warning, error, success — controlling icon, background, and timeline line color simultaneously. Fully designed and documented from scratch.

Reflection

What this work is teaching me.

Designing for logistics operators is designing for specialists. They know their domain far better than I do — my job is to understand their mental model well enough to get out of their way. A Booking form isn't an onboarding experience. The operator has done this hundreds of times. Every unnecessary click, every ambiguous label, every inconsistent pattern is friction that accumulates across their entire day.

The most valuable thing I've learned here is that complexity can't always be simplified — it can only be organized. A Booking has many fields because a real booking has many variables. The design challenge isn't reducing them; it's making them navigable, scannable, and consistent so the operator can move through them with confidence.

Seeing the Activity Event component go from a need I identified in the Booking sidebar to a documented, reusable part of the Design System — that's the kind of feedback loop I want to keep building: product work that makes the system better, and a system that makes the product work easier to build.
